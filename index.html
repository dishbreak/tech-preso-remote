Where did this talk come from?

Many moons ago, I was in charge of an internship program.

My job was to provide them curriculum and development.

(yes, as nebulous as it sounds)

I decided technical presentation was an important part of that task.

In a lot of ways, those interns were like you.

They were early in their technical careers.

They had novel and interesting ideas.

They were highly effective engineers.

But you're also different from them.

You routinely collaborate across teams.

You've got experience as an intern.

You're working from home during a global pandemic.

Is this talk still relevant?

Spoiler: I think so.

Let's go over the basics first.

The Three Elements of Technical Presentation

  1. The Audience. Who are you presenting to?
  2. The Story. What are you telling your audience?
  3. The Delivery. How will you tell your audience your story?

The Audience

What are the capacities and capabilities of the people you're presenting to?

What's the use case for your presentation?

How can your audience help you?

Your audience is the key driver.

The Story

How are you of use to your audience?

What are your core ideas?

What will you ask of your audience?

Focus on giving your audience as much value as possible.

The Delivery

Nobody wants to be a boring presenter.

Keep your tone light and conversational.

Use stories and narratives to illustrate and convey your point.

You're not presenting, you're just talking.

Working through a pandemic is hard.

A lot of us feel like ducks paddling in water.

There's more ways to get distracted.

Many of us have multiple roles to fill at home and at work.

Your audience has to work a lot harder.

Zoom presentations just aren't as interactive.

Shared physical space lets us transmit more nonverbal cues.

Delays of even a second hamper our communications.

It's even harder to "read the room" when it's not actually a room.

But, there is still some opportunity.

Challenging times force us to rethink things we normally just accept.

A virtual setting can be a chance for you to try new techniques!

Our need to communicate hasn't changed.

Manage your audience size.

Physical presentations have constraints.

There's only so many conference rooms.

You have to coordinate around when people are in the office.

The cost of physical presentations are a limiting factor.

With virtual presentations, we can invite everyone to everything!

Is that really a good idea, though?

Presentations are still expensive for attendees.

It's a huge drag for an attendee to be in a presentation they don't need to be in.

What can you do to help?

Manage the size of your audience.

Use the "optional" feature to let attendees know that they can skip if they want.

When you need to have a larger meeting, appoint an MC to run your chat and collect feedback.

Get your story straight.

Physical presentations can be relatively unstructured.

With the right group, you can have a productive meeting even if you have a loose agenda.

Ideation and brainstorming is easier when you have body language.

When you're all in the same room you can muddle through a meeting.

Virtual presentations need more structure.

Virtual presentations are inherently less interactive.

An unclear agenda or an unfocused discussion will push people to tune out.

Your audience needs to know what's expected of them.

Have a clear agenda for your presentation.

Set acceptance criteria for the presentation. What will make it a success?

Make it easier for people to follow up with the results of the discussion. Publish your slides, meeting notes, RFCs, etc.

Have fun out there!

The conversational style of delivery is so important.

Listening to other people talk isn't easy.

Your audience is on the verge of burnout.

What can you do to make things a little more fun and/or engaging?

Try for more interactivity in your presentations.

Build in points for discussion.

Consider basing your meeting on a document that people read before discussing.

Tell stories and make your audience members characters in them.

In Closing

It's harder out there for speakers and audiences.

Reduce your audience size where possible to keep things high and tight.

Put in the work to have organized presentations with a clear agenda.

Go the extra mile to make your talks interactive.
